Barangay Tabang

BDRRM Kwentuhan and Community Walk

Bryan Carlo T. Capulong 
University of Santo Tomas | NSTP CWTS C-IICS-10

 Two hours to the north of the University of Sto. Tomas, is my home and breathing ground, the Barangay Tabang in the province of Bulacan. At the forefront of the barangay, and a few-minute walk away from my house, is the Barangay Hall. A home to the respectable leaders who keep the barangay thriving, and a place of refuge to residents in distress. As I was searching for a resource person, I was blessed enough to be given a scheduled appointment for interview on the following day.

BDRRM Kwentuhan

Assessing Barangay Tabang's disaster situation, areas
of vulnerability, and disaster management capacity.

November 30, 2019, Saturday—Armored by the courage of the tiger and adorned by my identity as a Thomasian, I step into the hall to conduct my scheduled BDRRM Kwentuhan. To my relief, the room was filled with friendly and outgoing people who were hospitable to my presence. It was quite a humorous moment as they were passing to each other, the obligation to attend to my questions, showing that everyone was capable to do so. Ah yes, I am proud of my people. Finally, I had Secretary Jojo V. Marquez as my interviewee, from whom I was able to receive invaluable knowledge on the Barangay Tabang’s Disaster Risk Reduction and Management capabilities within a short, 13-minute session.

Community Walk

A journey to remember & worthy to share

Observations and reflections to the safe, hazardous, and wholesome qualities of my nurturing community.

My home, my
breathing ground,
Barangay Tabang

Together my father, who has been taking my pictures, we took to the streets of Barangay Tabang, Plaridel, Bulacan. With this community walk, I wanted to showcase some of my Barangay's best places and wholesome lifestyle. Through these images, I hope to relate with you the things that I love about my barangay as my ultimate safe haven and the best place to be in. However, like most things in this world, my barangay also has its own issues which need to be addressed.

Living the way we love: Peaceful, Safe and Wholesome

PreviousNext

First is the basketball court and tennis court! Having these two right next to each other makes it feel like a big field to let loose and be free from all the stresses that plagued the body throughout the course of the week. Part of the lifestyle of the people in Barangay Tabang is to engage in sports. With these two courts in the middle, it would be a waste not to do so! The early morning just after sunrise, and late afternoon just before the sun sets, are its busiest hours most particularly during the weekends. If you cannot bring friends, then meet new ones! The two courts are free and accessible to anyone and is a place to bring people closer together.

When it is not serving as a sports facility, these two courts are among the most open areas in Barangay Tabang which means that it is among the safest places that you could be in during an earthquake as it is free from possible falling objects and structures such as trees and electricity posts. 

PreviousNextClose

The Saturday Tyange. What makes this marketplace a bit different from the usual, is that it only sets up during Saturday mornings. It usually lasts only until 10 AM so do not come late! With prices so cheap and even bargainable, the people in Barangay Tabang would not dare miss the once-a-week tyange at the park.  With that said, coming here has become one of the best parts of the weekly lifestyle of the people in my barangay. In fact, in our experience, it is not rare to meet and greet your neighbors or friends while in the tyange as it really is something that we do not want to miss. It is not as crowded as the Manila marketplaces. There is enough space to walk comfortably in the middle of its street, and stopping to check a stall would not be much of a bother to others at all. With that, pickpockets are unlikely visitors, but of course, it still helps to be cautious with the things you carry. If you fear that an earthquake would occur as you shop here, then worry not! This is an open area at the park, with no structures that could fall over. Aside from that, it is right next to the basketball court and tennis court mentioned earlier.

The police station and barangay hall. As a barangay resident, it would crucially matter to be aware of the places to get help, and that is none other than the police station and the barangay hall. The friendly and respectable appointed officers of the police and barangay are highly capable in handling and settling issues brought up by the residents. It goes without saying that safety, peace and order are also within their top priority. When encountering issues involving other people, or problems with barangay infrastructure, the residents are enjoined to seek the aid of local authorities to professionally handle the case, as the best practice. In times of disasters, the two facilities are always available as places to seek refuge. The barangay officials, as mentioned in the BDRRM Kwentuhan, will accommodate and guide you to the available evacuation centers, as well as provide the necessary relief goods. Visiting these two places is an optimal approach in order to preserve the peace and order as well as to reduce the vulnerability of residents to being exposed to and harmed by hazards. It is not merely belittling the capacities of the residents themselves in dealing with such issues, but simply acknowledging the presence and role of local officials in promoting the welfare of its constituents.

The park playground. Situated near the police station is a playground where the youth of the community usually comes to unwind. While the kids prefer going on slides and seesaws as they actively play with friends, I personally prefer the serenity and solitude in this swinging bench-like structure. This place promotes a socially active lifestyle where the cheerful laughters, calm talks, and the playful sounds of metal are the music of the scenery. On the concern of safety, the local government ensures the park's maintenance and cleanliness to minimize the risk of physical harm or stimulating illnesses such as allergies. As always, it is advised that children be accompanied by someone older.

Street foods by the road. Near the entrance of our community, there are several street food vendors who welcome the residents, hungry and tired from daily appointments like school and work, with different foods like chicken provens, banana and camote cues, barbeques, buko juice, fish ball, tacos, and a lot more. Safety here is not much of a question, as the area is very open. On the other hand, control and moderation always proves to be a programmed decision in aiming for a good lifestyle while still having these types of food.

Other Best Practices

Roadside Trashbins

People carry snacks with cups, sticks, and wrappers which some habitually dispose of to the side of the road. Having trashbins at the side of the road encourages the people to quit the bad habit, and minimize the number of trash that litter the beauty of our community.

Designated Loading/Unloading Zones

These areas not only provide commuters with the convenience of easily finding a ride, it also gives us a safe waiting space at the side of the road, away from the busy and risky traffic and at company with other commuters. That is why it is important to encourage the practice to commuters and to strictly enforce the rule for drivers to load and unload only within these zones.

Sanitary Food Establishments

I have observed that the workplace cleanliness is maintained in the various food establishments in the barangay commercial area. A clearly admirable practice as it concerns the welfare of the customers who will be consuming the food that they prepare. Among these are the lines of lechon manok sellers which are common in the area.

Proper Waste Segregation

I commend efforts of establishments to provide segregated trash bins as it strongly helps the environment and also promotes public health. Moreover, separating hazardous wastes from the others helps to protect the residents and the collectors from being exposed to its hazard in the case of mishandling the hazardous waste.

Roadside Walls

The presence of these barriers make it safer for passing vehicles by blocking the risk of falling down the water, especially at night when it can be hard to see the road and the waters differently.

 

Use of Pedestrian Lanes

Crossing the road on pedestrian lanes is inarguably a best practice that everyone should strive to follow. Doing so gives pedestrians a right of way to go from one side to the other, which passing vehicles need to respect by stopping momentarily as the pedestrians pass by.


Community Issues: Hazards and Bad Practices

Irresponsible garbage disposal. This problem is very common and it is not new anymore, which is exactly why it is bad. Irresponsibly disposed garbage, whether a single piece or a big heap of it, is still an ensuing issue in many communities due to the bad habit of the people to litter. For some people, it is more convenient to just drop their trash on the ground or to the side of the road, than to look for or reach for a publicly available garbage bin. As people persist on throwing to a similar spot, the trash there accumulates into a heap that eventually becomes highly conspicious to the public eye. This is bad as seeing the amounts of trash may encourage other passersby to exhibit the same habit of littering. Trash lying around anywhere in the community grounds, is definitely an eyesore. Aside from that, it may also serve as a health hazard to the residents who may encounter it. The first thing to consider is the bacteria that may brood within the piles of garbage. As litterers would not really be choosy as to where they will throw their trash, improperly disposed garbage is not really segregated, and so it can be expected that a garbage pile can be a mix of both biodegradable and non-biodegradables. The bacteria in these trash may cause adverse health effects once it mixes with the air or water that the residents may have contact with through transporting media such as winds and floods. Cholera and typhoid are some of the diseases that may affect public health through this pollution (Rinkesh, n.d.). Solid waste such as tin cans or broken glass may also wound an unsuspecting person who may unknowingly step on them. In many cases, a prohibiting signage is not enough to prevent people from littering in an area. Local government units need to develop more effective ways to catch and penalize the disruptors of community cleanliness.   

Pagsisiga or burning of waste. Pagsisiga or the burning of solid wastes such as plastics and leaves, is another improper practice by which some people choose to dispose of their solid waste. The difference is that with burning, the solid waste is reduced to charred particles which may be less of an eyesore compared to seeing piles of trash in public places. Even though disposing in this way may produce cleaner results, it is considered as bad practice and is prohibited under Chapter VI Section 48 of R.A. 9003 or the Solid Waste Management Act which penalizes violators with a fine of three hundred pesos up to one thousand pesos, along with one up to 15 days imprisonment. Despite of this, I was still able to spot burning of wastes during my community walk, which was not much of a surprise to me anymore. An act like this often ensues because the instigators are not punished due to being uncaught. While the burner is away, he leaves his fire to affect passersby with air pollutants that irritate the eyes, skin, and respiratory functions. To prevent more of this, the people need to be more aware of how their act affects others and concern themselves with the welfare of their fellowmen.

Power lines near trees. Another potential source of hazard that I was able to spot during our community walk was that some of the power lines were touching against the branches and leaves of trees. This opens up a path for electricity to reach the ground, posing the risk of electocution or electrical burns to people who may come in close physical contact with the area (Unison Group, 2014). Action to cut down the overlapping parts of the tree is much needed from the property owner, if in a privately owned area, or from the local government officials, in order to eliminate these risks.

No helmet/mask policy. As a measure to keep track of persons included in the authorities' watchlist, and to prevent escapists from hiding their identity, an implementation in our locality urges riders to take off their helmets or masks. It is confusing and contradicting to the common rule that requires people to be equipped with helmets, both for the rider and the passenger. In fact, the Republic Act 10054 or the Motorcycle Helmet Act of 2009 strictly mandates the wearing of standard protective helmets for motorcycle riders, otherwise they would incur penalties. Without a helmet, riders are vulnerable to a high risk of incurring severe head injury in the event of an accident. On the other hand, the prohibition of wearing masks may affect a few people who have legitimate reasons for doing so, such as those with respiratory problems. With these, I think it would be rightful for the local government to think of a different policy which would not affect the persons free of guilt nor compromise the safety of its constituents.

Unsafe practice in construction sites. In every construction site, it must be a standardized procedure to wear personal protective equipment such as hard hats, protective gloves, and protective clothing. The first thing that I noticed about the people inside while I was looking from outside the scene, was they were not wearing hard hats which should be among the protective equipment that they should at least have. Additionally, as what can be seen from the image, there are randomly stacked building supplies which can turn into hazardous falling objects in accidental scenarios. From my short observation during our community walk, the site does not seem to promote much on safety. Although it is not much of a big construction project, I believe safety efforts should be a crucial requirement anywhere.
